About Me

I've been passionate about programming since I was 12 and have worked professionally as a software engineer for over 25 years. I've always loved solving puzzles, and for me, programming has always felt like that -- recognizing patterns, realizing the proper solution, and composing many simple pieces into a complex system. When people think of creative disciplines, they usually picture musicians, artists, or writers -- not programmers. But building a quality application demands as much imagination as technical skill.


After a period of personal exploration and renewal -- including learning many new skills, earning professional certifications, and developing a new portfolio app -- I'm excited to return to the field with fresh perspective and renewed focus.


Why You Need Me

AI has revolutionized the software development landscape, and will continue to do so for the forserable future. Like many other tools of past eras, it has enabled one person to do the work of many.

But also like any tool, you still need someone to wield it. At least today, you can't simply tell an AI to create an app for you, and then come back the next day and expect to find anything remotely usable. Anyone who tells you otherwise is trying to sell something. The stories I could tell you about some of the ways even top-tier AIs have responded to fairly straightforward requests would make for a pretty good comedy series.

More than ever, you need someone with experience and insight at the helm to spot an AI's mistakes and keep it on course, especially considering how completely confident they are in even their most outrageous hallucinations. You need someone like me.

CrossCracker

A crossword puzzle solver.

CrossCracker (play demo video)

CrossCracker uses AI to solve crossword puzzles.

  • The latest version can solve a New York Times puzzle with no errors (watch the video!).
  • Back end is Python, front end is HTML/JavaScript/CSS, connected by FastAPI-hosted REST and websocket APIs.
  • Uses an LLM for candidate answer generation, evaluation, and validation.
  • Capable of using any Ollama-hosted LLM locally, or any Anthropic LLM over the Internet.
  • Uses a ChromaDB database of crossword hints for Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), substantially improving solution accuracy.
  • When running a local LLM, I prefer the Llama 3.1 (8b) model. It provides the best compromise between size, speed, and effectiveness. And it's free!
  • When using one of the big LLMs available over the Internet, Claude Opus 4.6 is the current state of the art. It is MUCH more effective than anything I could ever run locally -- but it isn't free. Each puzzle solution costs $0.50-1.00 in token usage, so... best saved for demo videos and special occasions.
  • CrossCracker 2.0 is vastly more capable than version 1.0. Partly that's due to much-improved program logic, and partly due to the use of a ChromaDB "hint" database. Retrieval-Augmented Geneation (RAG) using vector databases is the new normal for AI apps, and for good reason.
  • Puzzle aficionados will know that the New York Times crossword is the gold standard, and that it is easiest on Monday and gets harder throughout the week. With the Llama LLM, CrossCracker 2 can reliably solve a Monday NYT puzzle. With Opus it can reliably solve a Tuesday NYT puzzle and could probably solve even a Sunday puzzle if it only had to answer clues, but after Tuesday the NYT puzzles generally have all kinds of gimmicks like rebuses and themes and self-referential clues that my solver is not equipped to handle. Maybe some day.
  • The algorithm and heuristics for selecting which answer to place, and more importantly, which one to backtrack when stuck, was actually far more difficult than integrating the AI. I'm confident that no AI in the world could generate the code that I designed and wrote for this solver. At least, not yet. Claude Opus sure couldn't -- when asked to try, it produced a lot of unmaintainable, bug-ridden spaghetti code. So yes, there's still a place in software engineering for us puny humans.
